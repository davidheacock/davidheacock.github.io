TUNA FISHING AND REMOTE SENSING

COMMERCIAL TUNA FISHING


Over the last several decades the demand for tuna has increased rapidly around the world. The tuna market alone accounts for over $42 billion of the global economy – a figure greater than the GDP of at least 108 countries.1 With such high demand and competition, tuna populations have faced high volatility, sparking great concern from scientists, politicians, and businessmen who seek to preserve the species’ place in markets and ecosystems.



Video Source: BBC Two: Tuna Fishing - South Pacific


ABOUT TUNA:






    Tuna are powerful, migratory fish that are constantly on the hunt for their next meal. Each day they consume roughly a quarter of their bodyweight in forage fish which include sardines, sand lances, and sauries.2
    Migration patterns for tuna can be thousands of miles long and span multiple continents. There are over 70 countries that harvest the species, making it a challenge to validate catch numbers and coordinate regulatory efforts.3
    The scombridae family of fish, to which tuna belong, fell by 74% between 1970 and 2012. outpacing a decline of 49% for 1,234 ocean species over that same time.4
    The Atlantic bluefin tuna, known for its delicacy status in Japanese cuisine, has fallen to just 2.6% of its historic size, however, two major global fisheries commissions recently agreed to tighten regulations on bluefin tuna with hopes of restoring the population to 20% of its historic size by 2034.5
REMOTE SENSING

WHAT IS REMOTE SENSING?

Remote Sensing is the science of collecting and interpreting data about the surface of the earth from a distance using sensors that capture electromagnetic radiation, sound waves, or other forms of energy. Most people are familiar with using aerial images for navigation, or radar maps to observe weather patterns, however there are many other useful applications of remote sensing to study the world around us.

While we can't just look at high resolution satellite images and see where tuna are swimming, we can observe several environmental features that strongly correlate to optimal tuna habitats. Click through the slideshow below to learn more about remote sensing and how we can use it to help us monitor tuna populations.


While satellites may be miles above the earth's surface, their constant output of data gives us a unique look into global environmental trends. Some satellites, such as Landsat, capture imagery of the entire earth. Others, such as SeaWIFS, focus solely on the ocean and its biological data.

MODIS

ABOUT MODIS:

MODIS (or Moderate Resolution Imaging Spectroradiometer) serves as the sensory instrument aboard NASA's Terra and Aqua satellites. Terra and Aqua work in tandem to orbit the earth, passing each area roughly three hours apart optimizing cloud-free viewing opportunities.6 The entire world surface is covered in 1-2 days in 36 discrete spectral bands including visible light and infrared radiation.7 Between the two sensors, nearly 500 million images are collected every day, providing scientists with an incredible amount of data to observe environmental changes around the world.8


Image credit: NASA

The animation above shows the Aqua satellite traveling in its sun-synchronous, near-polar orbit. It circles the earth every 99 minutes, passing within 10 degrees of each pole on every orbit. The whitish, opaque imagery that appears around the satellite represents its swath - the earth area captured by the sensor during its pass



Image credit: Barbara Muhling (SFSC-NOAA)

Studies like the one shown above demonstrate how MODIS imagery combined with field data and scientific analysis can play a major role in how we study the health of our planet. With such a broad scope of observation required to tackle a challenge like tracking the global tuna population, MODIS imagery becomes a clear and necesarry asset when in search of helpful data.
